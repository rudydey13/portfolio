Assessing Market Concentration in US Banking ¶

The file "bank-data.csv" contains data on the number of commercial banking institutions, branches, and offices in the United States at the end of each year between 1934 to 2019. (This data is maintained by the Federal Deposit Insurance Corporation (FDIC) and can be downloaded from https://banks.data.fdic.gov/. The FDIC has a lot of interesting banking data. You are encouraged to browse around their website to see what's there.)

The history of U.S bankng, commercial or investment, and their relationship with the government, is extremely long and complicated. However, in order to get a preliminary understanding of the graphs above, there are 4 specific laws that should be looked at. The very first act to be looked at is the Glass-Steagall Act, which was signed after the 1929 stock market crash by President Franklin D. Rosevelt. The Glass-Steagall Act, along with a few other smaller acts, implemented regulation on deposit interest rates, and separated commercial banking from investment banking. Hence, in Figure 1 there is a sharp increase in the number of commercial banks in the U.S right after the passage of the Glass-Steagall. This sharp increase was essentially artifically created since the Glass-Steagall act simply separated the banks into commercial and investment.

Since the Glass-Steagall Act, the total number of U.S commercial banks remained relatively the same (with banks steadily increasing the number of offices they had -- refer to Figure 2) until the 1980s when the number started to decline. The banks had started losing market share to other financial institutions, and so in 1980 Congress passed the Depository Institutions Deregulation and Monetary Control Act (DIDMCA). This act deregulated the financial insitutions that accept deposits while strengthening the Federal Reserve's control over Monetary policy. This act accelerated the move towards consolidation and conglomeration of the commercial banks. This can be seen in Figure 1: the decline in the number of commercial banks begins shortly after the passage of the DIDMCA.

In addition to the DIDMCA, in 1994 Congress passed the Riegle-Neal Interstate Banking Act. This act allowed banks to cross state line and establish offices under a national uniform standard. THE DIDMCA led to the decrease in commercial banking companies and the Riegle-Neal Act allowed the remaining commercial banking companies to increase the number of offices they had, and thus the average number of offices per bank started increasing at a much faster rate after the passage of the Riegel-Neal Act compared to before -- refer to Figure 3.

Finally, referring to Figure 2, there is a clear decrease in the number of offices of commercial banks around 2008. This decrease can be attributed to the Great Recession along with the passage of the Dodd-Frank Act. As a result of the Great Recession, a number of large banks had to close numerous offices around the country, and the passage of the Dodd-Frank Act further regulated the industry and forced the big banks to offload a number of the smaller offices/banks they owned around the country.
